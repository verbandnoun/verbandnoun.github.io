In a recent round of research with participants, I’ve slipped in a few questions about how they define success, and then how their organization defines success.


Why do I do this sick and twisted thing? Because I think it matters. Think about it: I was cleaning the dishes this evening. And if a researcher was doing a task analysis on me, they would find that I have a sequence of steps that help me acheive dish cleaning.


But, why do I even clean the dishes? It’s not for the sake of the dishes. I clean them because success looks like a glass of wine and listening to Miles Davis after I’m done. It’s also the feeling of having a clean kitchen which makes my wife happy.

I’ve Been Trying This Thing

 

Subheading

I’ve asked research particpants to tell me about how they define success. This isn’t a direct question either. Sometimes, I’ll ask a participant, “In your role, what is something that makes you feel like you’ve knocked it out of the park? Like you’ve really done your job well?”

Conversly, I also ask, “What keeps you up at night? What does a rubbish day look like for you?”


What knowing this does for the design process is gives the designers a point of empathy and perspective to examine their work from. “How does this flow make the user successful?”


Cleaning the dishes is only a bare minimum of what success looks like for me on a Wednesday night. Success is relaxing at home.
