When I was 16 I worked at Coldstone Creamery. It was for the most part a great job. I could eat ice cream for free, there was always stuff to do during my shift and I really liked my coworkers. After a few months of working there in the afternoons, I noticed something: every customer that came in was happy. Dads brought their daughters in and they giggled at each others orders. Or a couple would come in after a dinner date and share a big, sumptious Sunday.

People Don’t Go for Ice Cream When They’re Pissed.

An experience has context. And with that context comes an array of qualities. One being emotion. When I realized that everyone that came into the ice cream shop was in a great mood, it struck me that the nature of an ice cream shop is that it’s something people tend towards when they’re enjoying themselves or want to enjoy themselves (Side note: People are better tippers when in a good  mood).



Take this idea and extend it to another situation: going to the hospital. At the hospital, the tone of a person changes drastically. They tend to be sullen or panicked. This can also be true of the staff. They’ve dealt with crisis of varying levesl all day and night. And these qualities of the context tend to influence how the product they’re using will work. Buttons may need to be larger and communicate more urgency with color. Information is more data-dense but also critical (if you give the patient the wrong meds, you could be in trouble…).


I guess what I’m trying to get at is, sense the emotion of your users when they’re using your product in a certain context:


• Finacial experiences maybe more serious and precise and frustrating.

• Social experiences could be more jublient and are maybe helping someone pass the time.

• Workstation contexts may have noise and there maybe high emotion and the need to focus. So maybe text needs to be bigger or there needs to be multiple actions for each object in the task flow.
